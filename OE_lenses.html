Codename: LENSES

Purchasing the right camera is important, but lenses are arguably the most essential part to any camera rig. Lenses are a heavy factor in determining the quality and usability of footage captured by a camera. We will discuss the differences, advanatages, and disadvantages of the following lenses:

  • Zoom lenses
  • Prime lenses
  • Standard lenses
  • Macro lenses
  • Telephoto lenses
  • Fisheye lenses
  • Wide angle lenses

Zoom Lenses

A zoom lens is a lens that can cover a variety of focal lengths within its specified range. For instance, a 24-70mm zoom lens means that with that lens, you can zoom in and out to any focal length between 24mm and 70mm. This allows a camera to capture imagery in nearby surroundings and have the capability to zoom in into subjects or scenes that are further away, all without changing the position of the camera or moving one’s body around! Since a zoom lens covers multiple focal lengths, it could be a substitute for several prime lenses; it can be considered to have the focal capabilities of several prime lenses into one lens. Sometimes the downside to zoom lenses are they tend to be rather bulkier than prime lenses, as well as more expensive since they cover multiple focal lengths. Zoom lenses can also sometimes produce lesser quality imagery than prime lenses, since most prime lenses have higher lower aperture numbers (which means it can take in more light = better quality imagery) and they are specially made to do outstanding work in their specified focal length.

Prime Lenses

Prime lenses are also known as fixed-length lenses, referring to the fact prime lenses can only operate from the single focal length they were designed for; i.e. a 35mm lens can only work at 35mm, lacking the ability to zoom in or zoom out. Prime lenses are known to produce sharper quality images since they are designed to cater to their specified focal length. Prime lenses typically also have wider apertures, allowing more light into the camera, becoming very useful in low-light situations. Where prime lenses lack is in their inability to zoom, meaning that the camera would have to physically be moved to capture a scene/subject from a different distance field of view. They are typically also more lightweight and space-efficient than zoom lenses.

Wide Angle Lenses

Wide angle lenses are the grouping of lenses with shorter focal ranges; typical wide angle lens are between 24mm-35mm, and ultra wide angle lenses have typical focal lengths between 8mm-24mm. These lenses are used to capture a wider scene or subject, being especially useful in capturing imagery such as landscapes, areas with limited spacing, and buildings.

Standard Lenses

A standard lens refers to a grouping of lenses that are reminiscent of the natural field of view from the human eye, so they typically have a mid-range focal length, most often between 35mm-85mm. Standard lenses are typically prime lenses, which denotes they have usually wider apertures, produce better quality imagery, and perform exceptionally in low-light conditions. Standard lenses are most often used for portraits, pictures that flatter the face of a person or persons.

Telephoto Lenses

Telephoto lenses are the grouping of lenses geared towards long distance photography; short distance telephoto lens are typically around the 85mm-125mm focal lengths, mid-distance telephoto lens typically range between 135mm-300mm, and super telephoto lenses typically range 300mm and up. Telephoto lenses help in cases where your subject/scene can’t be approached up close so it requires a lens that can zoom into the scene without physically entering the scene.

Macro Lenses

Macro lens are the grouping of lenses specifically engineered for close-up photography. These lenses are ideal for product photography and promotions, as well as any form of photography that requires a lens to be pay attention to fine details. They require a photographer to get close to the subject at hand to produce the best imagery.

Fisheye Lenses

Fisheye lenses can be considered a special form of wide angle lens that has a rounded field of vision, typically around 180 degrees. The image created looks completely distorted since it has a 180 degree field of view, and is considered more so for creative filming rather than more serious subjects of film. Due to the fact it creates a sort of fishbowl effect, its mostly used when a peculiar perspective is required for a scene/subject.
